Subtheme: Visualizing Exclusionary Zoning

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. How has the “executive belt” surrounding Boston changed? Do these areas still have extensive single-family zoning? — Recently, there have been strong efforts to change zoning laws and prioritize transit-oriented development. Have we started to see any changes in municipalities previously aligned with the "executive belt"? Have any such municipalities drastically overhauled their restrictive zoning?
  2. Are there certain municipalities that have higher fractions of non-white residents? Do municipalities with higher fractions have less single-family zoning? Are there observable differences between municipalities in terms of income or number of children? — Amy Dain extensively talks about municipalities historically using zoning laws to exclude based on class and race. I am curious to what extent those laws and impacts persist today.
  3. How are rail network access and municipal housing composition related? — Public transit and dense housing are often considered as symbiotic, where denser housing necessitates improved transit and improved transit encourages denser housing. Accordingly, I wonder how the stated growth policies and municipal zoning restrictions correspond to MBTA rail service, particularly with regards to dates of development, routes available, and frequency of service.

Discoveries & Insights

The dataset provided by The Warren Group consists of information on residential zoning, demographics, and household income and composition for municipalities served by the Boston Metropolitian Area Planning Council. Prior to engaging with this data, I read Exclusionary by Design by Amy Dain. This report details the history of exclusionary zoning practices in Greater Boston, including fiscal, class, and racist zoning, since the 1920's. My analysis will focus on similar themes. Before approaching my overall analysis questions, I began with a brief review of data quality. The first thing I discovered was that some municipalities are duplicated in the dataset. For such municipalities, the data on areas zoned for single-family housing is recorded separately and notated by a flag. These single-family-only datasets were filtered out using this flag; the analysis to follow focuses on whole municipalities. When considering flat population numbers, large municipalities such as Boston and Cambridge skew the results. Therefore, rather than filter out certain municipalities, I will use population fractions or percentages extensively throughout this analysis.

Summary

There is a long history of excusionary zoning in the Greater Boston area. This history includes four major areas, according to Amy Dain's Exclusionary by Design: original adoption (1920's and 1930's), postwar era (up to approximately 1968), The Big Downzone (1968-1975), and the current era (1975 - present). The most relevant areas for my analysis are as follows: i) the post war era, where municipalities tightened zoning requirements and some affluent communities formed an "estate belt" by "[zoning] for very large minimum lot sizes for single-family homes and mostly banned multifamily housing" and ii) The Big Downzone, where communities responded to desegregation and activist pushes to reform zoning by "shifting to official policies of 'no growth' or 'low growth'".

The effects of these zoning policies are still evident today. I showed that strict zoning laws still exist in a ring surounding Boston, although some municipalities (particularly those near Downtown Boston) have zero single-family zoning. Further, racial/ethnic segregation persists today, where many municipalities, and particularly those with strict single-family zoning, are predominantly white and disproportionately have higher household incomes. This segregation is most pronounced for black and hispanic/latino communities; these populations are concentrated in a few municipalities. Asian populations, however, are more evenly distributed throughout the Greater Boston area; the different outcomes for asian and black/hispanic populations would be an interesting topic for future work.
