Andrew Scott White — aswhite@mit.edu

Subtheme: Visualizing Exclusionary Zoning

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. How has the “executive belt” surrounding Boston changed? Do these areas still have extensive single-family zoning?
  2. Are there certain municipalities that have higher fractions of non-white residents? Do municipalities with higher fractions have less single-family zoning? Are there observable differences in income, household size, and number of children?
  3. Which municipalities that sought “no growth” achieved it? How much did they grow/evolve since the 1970's?
  4. How are rail network access and municipal housing composition related?

Discoveries & Insights

The dataset provided by The Warren Group consists of information on residential zoning, demographics, and household income and composition for municipalities served by the Boston Metropolitian Area Planning Council. Prior to engaging with this data, I read Exclusionary by Design by Amy Dain. This report details the history of exclusionary zoning practices in Greater Boston, including fiscal, class, and racist zoning, since the 1920's. My analysis will focus on similar themes. Before approaching my overall analysis questions, I began with a brief review of data quality. The first thing I discovered was that some municipalities are duplicated in the dataset. For such municipalities, the data on areas zoned for single-family housing is recorded separately and notated by a flag. After filtering out the separate single-family data, I began by looking at the percentage of residential land zoned for single-family housing in each municipality, as shown in Figure 1. My first takeaway is that many municipalities still employ extensive single-family zoning — 48 municipalities zone the majority of residential land for single-families, marked by those above the dashed line. Next, I noticed the group of municipalities with zero single-family zoning. I plotted the same data on a map, as shown in Figure 2, to look for any spatial trends. I plotted with three color bins to highlight regions with low, moderate, and high percentages of single-family zoning. The first feature I notice is the ring of red, which in 1960 the Boston Globe dubbed as the "Executive Belt". This ring of affluent communities maintained their status through the use of exclusionary zoning techniques. In following figures, we will further address the first two analysis questions with these in mind. Before we assess the demographics of each municipality, I wanted to focus on the estates explicitly mentioned as part of the "Executive Belt" in Amy Dain's report. Accordingly, I filtered the results from the previous map to show only these municipalities in Figure 3. I was surprised to see that multiple municipalities were recorded as having zero single-family zoning: Wayland, Carlisle, Westwood, and Hamilton. The rest of the executive municipalities maintained very high percentages of single-family zoning. To verify this result, I looked into the zoning of Wayland specifically and found a district map showing they are almost entirely zoned for single-family housing (Wayland Inc., 2019). This result is in conflict with the dataset provided my The Warren Group, which brings into question the validity of the data in this report. Future work should be done to validate the single-family housing percentages, particularly for the "Executive Belt" municipalities shown here.
