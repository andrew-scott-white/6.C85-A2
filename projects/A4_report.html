Proposition: a few large, industrialized nations are disproportionately contributing to the climate crisis through their emission of carbon dioxide. More generally, nations in the global north (positive latitudes, north of the equator) are responsible for over 90% of carbon dioxide emissions.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • The decision to show national emissions, as opposed to per capita, highlights the impact that industrialized nations have. Often, people attempt to deflect the blame onto individuals despite companies being largely responsible for this pollution. This chart answers that by showing the disproportionate impact that a few nations have. Seeing that China and the United States occupy nearly half the figure is intended to evoke an emotional, perhaps embarassed, but I would not call it deceptive [1].
  • I also chose to show the global north and south, defined by positive and negative lines of latitude. The intent is to show that the global south is disproportionately harmed relative to their contributions. The choice of color, red for the one I am calling out and blue for the one harmed, it certainly affective. I wouldn't say this is deceptive, but I will rank it in that direction [-0.5].
  • The annotation is meant to respond to the unintended conclusion of "wow, China is the problem", which is partially fueled by my decision to show national magnitudes not per capita. China has over four times the population of the United States but only three times as many carbon emissions. As such, I placed the caption such that it draws the eyes towards the United States, where I explain that multiple other nations also contribute greatly to the problem. The viewer might next notice the caption in the largest blue square, which emphasizes that the global south is disproportionately harmed relative to their contribution. I would call these decisions earnest [1.5].

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • By considering emissions per capita, I am amplifying the contributions of nations with small populations. This effect is further exacerbated by the inclusion of land use changes. Forestation can offset substantial carbon dioxide emissions, so small island nations or regions with little forest coverage look much worse than a similar nation with forests. This decision makes the split between north and south a bit more even, and it downplays the effects of the populous, industrialized nations [-1].
  • I placed a caption in bolded red text (unfairly) blaming the people of the Solomon Islands for causing the climate crisis. This choice leans into the viewers tendenacy to focus on the largest square, which I leverage to make this nation a scapegoat [-2].
  • The source caption in the bottom left is an attempt to lend credibility to my conclusions. I note the source as an international (seemingly unbiased) fund that is focused on climate change! Surely they know what they are talking about. This choice is incredibly deceptive because it seems like my figure comes from them when, in reality, they only provided the dataset. They do not necessarily agree with my conclusions here [-2].

Final Reflection

Overall, this exercise was useful and enjoyable! It was difficult trying to figure out how to express the same dataset in polar opposite directions. My approach was to figure out how I felt about the data and drawing some conclusions. Then, I thought about how I might argue against myself. My intention with these charts is to get at the arguments about who is responsible for emissions. In my first chart, I argue that large, industrial nations are largely responsible. In the latter, I employ corporate rhetorical tactics to put the blame on the individual and take it even further by faulting a small island nation that i) ultimately contributes very little to the problem and ii) is most threatened by climate change due to rising sea levels.

This project has further solidified by view that data can be made to say anything. Every choice has some intention behind it, even when not being intentionally deceptive. Further, some might consider decisions I made to be deceptive if they have a different background or perspective. Others might draw conclusions that I did not intend, and they will see my work as supporting a different narrative. For example, one might conclude that China is the problem and no one else should bother reducing emissions until they do. I try to "head off" this argument through the use and positioning of captions. I attempt to draw the viewers attention to guide them to a more nuanced perspective: large nations like China and the United States are the problem. No one nation is responsible for the problem, but some nations contribute more than others.
